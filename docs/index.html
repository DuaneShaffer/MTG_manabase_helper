The gap most lists ignore

Spells are the easy part. The mana is the math.

You picked twenty-three great cards and then sleeved up whatever lands tapped for the right colors. But a manabase either delivers your colors on the turn you cast a card — or it doesn't, and the game decides it for you.

Manabase answers the one question your list can't: given these lands, how often do you actually cast this card on curve?

What you get

Read your mana like the spells are already cast.

Card-by-card grades

Every colored spell gets two numbers: the odds its colors are ready on the turn you cast it, and a simulated rate that folds in mana screw and flood. Both cap at 99% — never a fake guarantee.

The right land count

Not a rule of thumb. Your curve sets the number, trimmed for cheap card draw and ramp, clamped to where your archetype actually lives.

A base, recommended

One click solves for a concrete set — untapped sources first, taplands capped, held to your land target. When every color won't fit that count, it says which one gives and what to change.

Simulate real games

Play out thousands of shuffles, mulligans and draws — mana screw and flood included — for an on-curve rate the closed-form math can't capture. Still a model, with its own simplifications, but a more realistic one.

Lands that think

Duals, choose-a-color lands, and conditional fixers that only count their colors when your deck actually turns them on.

Under the hood

Built on Karsten — clear about where we differ.

Color requirements use Frank Karsten's conditional-hypergeometric source counts and his sliding (89 + mana value)% on-curve target — the framework competitive players cite.

We adapt it rather than reprint it, so our numbers can differ from his published tables: a Monte-Carlo simulator adds real mana screw and flood, multicolor cards get a joint-probability grade instead of his +1-source shortcut, the land-count formula caps its draw/ramp discount, and an ILP solver picks the actual lands.
